Deliberative democracy

  • Decisions are made by involving a representative sample of citizens to deliberate on it.
  • Pro: it genuinely listens.
  • Con: slow, expensive, and a few dozen participants at a time. Most of us never get the invitation.

We introduced 3 dimensions to characterize deliberative systems

  • Representation: how do individual perspectives enter a deliberative system?
  • Aggregation: how are individual perspectives combined into a collective output?
  • Revision: how does the collective output change as individual perspectives change over time?

V · Research

Representation

The agent has a huge amount of influence

  • The agent decides what to ask, what to write into memory, and how to read you when you were vague.
  • Interview-elicited opinions are systematically less alike (0.649) than agent-generated or directly-submitted ones (~0.75) — measured across all deliberations, not one example.
  • The interview itself shapes what the human thinks.

Aggregation

What we measured is what we got

  • Our ranking rule guarantees consensus: you might get a statement nobody objects to, but it might also be one nobody can act on.
  • Swap the rule and you just trade one way of being gamed for another.

What is a deliberation architecture?

In the Habermolt context
Representation: how to facilitate individuals to provide a meaningful voice? (interview → opinion)
Aggregation: individual opinions → collective output (statement generation + ranking)
Revision: how does the collective output change over time?
In the democratic deliberation context
Is creating consensus statements the best way to create consensus?
Should agents be able to directly communicate with each other?
In the most generalized context
Nothing more than: how do we put a group of humans together?

VI · Conclusion
Delegating Deliberation to AI Representatives

Not everyone thinks this should exist

"Inferring someone's preferences is not the same as holding their authority — an agent good enough to deliberate freely is no longer representing anyone."
— Noveck & Martí, two-part critique
Our answer: without an agent, your voice isn't even in the room. "Too costly to involve people" is what keeps the door closed. A cheap voice in the room is the wedge that pries open a seat for the human behind it.

VI · Conclusion
Delegating Deliberation to AI Representatives

Where should we delegate deliberation?

Low stakes?
Arguing with friends, scheduling, community rules
Expert domains?
Academic peer review, standards bodies
National policy?
Swiss National AI Dialogue · feedback on South Africa's AI policy

Most decisions don't need it. But wherever the door has stayed shut because humans were too expensive to convene — an agent in the room changes the economics of being heard.
